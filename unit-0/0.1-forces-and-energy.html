force

Position is the distance of something from a chosen point of reference. Displacement is the change from initial to final position. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement with respect to time. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time.

A force is a push or pull. Pretty simple. If I pull on a crate, I am exerting a force on that crate. If molecule A repels molecule B, molecule A exerts a force on molecule B. Mathematically, force can be positive or negative. A positive force points in the positive direction and a negative force points in the negative direction. We’ll talk about these directions in the context of an atom later. Because force has both direction and magnitude, we call it a vector. The net force acting on something is basically the vector sum of all the forces that act on it. A vector sum differs from a regular sum in that we add according to the directions.

Newton discovered a bunch of things about forces. He came up with three laws.

  1. Something at rest will remain resting and something moving (at constant velocity) will remain moving (with the same constant velocity) unless a net external force acts on it.
  2. \(F = ma\)
    • \(F =\) force
    • \(m =\) mass
    • \(a =\) acceleration
  3. For every force, there’s an equal (in magnitude) and opposite (in direction) force.

The units for force are Newtons. From Newton’s second law, we know that this is equivalent to \(\mathrm{kg} ~ \mathrm{m} ~ \mathrm{s^{-2}}\).

The main type of force that we deal with in chemistry is the electrostatic force, or the force that exists between stationary charges. The electrostatic force between two point charges is expressed in Coulomb’s Law: \[ F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \]

  • \(F =\) force
  • \(k = 9 \cdot 10^{9} \mathrm{~ N ~ m^2 ~ C^{-2}} = \) Coulomb's constant
  • \(q_1 =\) charge of one point charge
  • \(q_1 =\) charge of the other point charge
  • \(r =\) distance between the two point charges

This equation is pretty important and will be referenced a bunch of times later on. Memorize it. It tells us a few pretty important things that we can apply to atoms:

  • More charge = more force. Basically, using charges of higher magnitudes results in a force of a higher magnitude as well. Force and charge (of each point charge) is directly proportional.
  • Less distance = more force. In other words, the closer the two charges are, the greater the force between them is. We see that decreasing distance in the equation lowers the value of the denominator, resulting in a greater magnitude.
  • Forces between two like-charges are repulsive. Let’s examine this using cases:
    • Case 1: We have two positive charges. Multiplying \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) together results in a positive number since both are positive. Thus, force is also positive since neither Coulomb’s constant or the distance are negative.
    • Case 2: We have two negative charges. Multiplying \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) still results in a positive number because negative times negative is positive. Same as before, force is positive.
    This is pretty important as we’ll see in the next section. Remember it.
  • Forces between two unlike charges are attractive. This time when we multiply \(q_1\) by \(q_2\), we get a negative number, so force is negative as well. Also important.

energy

Anyways, being forced to move on, energy is the capacity for change, especially in the context of chemistry.

We usually talk about two types of energy: kinetic and potential. Potential energy is the energy something has based on how it’s arranged or configured. Let’s take gravity! Objects on Earth have a specific energy associated with them due to being in Earth’s gravitational field. We represent this using the equation \[U_g = mgh. \]

  • \(U_g = \) gravitational potential energy
  • \(m = \) mass
  • \(g = 9.81 \mathrm{~ m ~ s^{-2}} = \) gravitational acceleration

Where you starting measuring height (i.e., where \(h=0\)) is arbitrary and up to you. From this equation, we see that a higher height results in more energy. Hopefully this makes sense. The higher something is, the more its speed can change if it falls (and consequently, the more you’ll get hurt if it hits your head).

Kinetic energy is the energy associated with moving. Something has energy when it moves with some velocity. \[K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\]

  • \(K = \) kinetic energy
  • \(m = \) mass
  • \(v = \) velocity

A higher velocity means that you have higher kinetic energy. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of something. Whatever you’re taking the temperature of (probably) is made up of molecules. Molecules made up of atoms. Each atom with their own kinetic energy from vibrating and moving around a bit. The “average” here is the average across all these little atoms. So, a higher temperature means that the constituent molecules and atoms have more kinetic energy associated with vibration and motion. In other words, they move around faster.

Let's look at the units of energy. The SI units are joules. From looking at the two equations for potential energy, we see that this is equivalent to \[\mathrm{energy} = [\mathrm{kg}][\mathrm{m ~ s^{-2}}][\mathrm{m}].\] For kinetic energy, \[\mathrm{energy} = [\mathrm{kg}][\mathrm{m ~ s^{-1}}]^2.\] From these two, we see that energy generally simplifies into \(\mathrm{kg ~ m^2 ~ s^{-2}}\). This is equivalent to Joules (\(\mathrm{J}\)), which is the SI unit for energy.

Note: brackets are used (like above) to write down units and to figure out how they work in an equation. Also, don't actively memorize what units Joules are equal to.
