force

A force is a push or pull. If I pull on a crate, I am exerting a force on that crate. If molecule A repels molecule B, molecule A exerts a force on molecule B. Because force has both direction and magnitude, it is a vector. The net force acting on something is the vector sum of all the forces that act on it.

Newton discovered a bunch of things about forces. He came up with three laws:

  1. Something at rest will remain resting and something moving (at constant velocity) will remain moving (with the same constant velocity) unless a net external force acts on it.
  2. \(\sum F = ma\)
    • \(\sum F =\) net force \((\mathrm{Newtons, ~ N})\)
    • \(m =\) mass \((\mathrm{kilograms, ~ kg})\)
    • \(a =\) acceleration \((\mathrm{m ~ s^{-2}})\)
  3. For every force, there’s an equal (in magnitude) and opposite (in direction) force.

The units for force are Newtons. From Newton’s second law, we know that this is equivalent to \(\mathrm{kg} ~ \mathrm{m} ~ \mathrm{s^{-2}}\).

The main type of force that we deal with in chemistry is the electrostatic force, or the force that exists between stationary charges. The electrostatic force between two point charges is expressed in Coulomb’s Law: \[ F = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \]

  • \(F =\) force \((\mathrm{Newtons, ~ N})\)
  • \(k = 9 \cdot 10^{9} \mathrm{~ N ~ m^2 ~ C^{-2}} = \) Coulomb's constant
  • \(q_1 =\) charge of one point charge \((\mathrm{Coulombs, ~ C})\)
  • \(q_2 =\) charge of the other point charge \((\mathrm{Coulombs, ~ C})\)
  • \(r =\) distance between the two point charges \((\mathrm{meter, ~ m})\)

This equation is pretty important and will be referenced a bunch of times later on. It tells us a few pretty important things that we can apply to atoms:

  • More charge \(\to\) more force. Basically, using charges of higher magnitudes results in a force of a higher magnitude as well. Force and charge (of each point charge) are directly proportional.
  • Less distance \(\to\) more force. In other words, the closer the two charges are, the greater the force between them is. We see that decreasing distance in the equation lowers the value of the denominator, resulting in a greater magnitude.

The reason for this is unfortunately something that dives deep into quantum field theory. Hopefully the analogy to magnets makes it easier to believe.

Additionally, like charges (positive-positive or negative-negative) are repulsive, and unlike charges (positive-negative) are attractive. Magnets hold a similar property as well. Magnets repel when you try holding two of the same poles together (north-north or south-south) and attract if you hold opposite poles together (north-south). energy

Anyways, being forced to move on, energy is the capacity for change, especially in the context of chemistry.

We usually talk about two types of energy: kinetic and potential. Potential energy is the energy something has based on how it’s arranged or configured. Let’s take gravity! Objects on Earth have a specific energy associated with them due to being in Earth’s gravitational field. We represent this using the equation \[U_g = mgh. \]

  • \(U_g = \) gravitational potential energy \((\mathrm{Joules, ~ J})\)
  • \(m = \) mass \((\mathrm{kilograms, ~ kg})\)
  • \(g = 9.81 \mathrm{~ m ~ s^{-2}} = \) gravitational acceleration
  • \(h =\) height \((\mathrm{meter, ~ m})\)

Where you starting measuring height (i.e., where \(h=0\)) is arbitrary and up to you. From this equation, we see that a higher height results in more energy. Hopefully this makes sense. The higher something is, the more its speed can change if it falls (and consequently, the more you’ll get hurt if it hits your head).

Kinetic energy is the energy associated with moving. Something has energy when it moves with some velocity. \[K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\]

  • \(K = \) kinetic energy \((\mathrm{Joules, ~ J})\)
  • \(m = \) mass \((\mathrm{kilograms, ~ kg})\)
  • \(v = \) velocity \((\mathrm{m ~ s^{-1}})\)

A higher velocity means that you have higher kinetic energy. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of something. Whatever you’re taking the temperature of (probably) is made up of molecules. Molecules made up of atoms. Each atom with their own kinetic energy from vibrating and moving around a bit. The “average” here is the average across all these little atoms. So, a higher temperature means that the constituent molecules and atoms have more kinetic energy associated with vibration and motion. In other words, they move around faster.

Brackets are sometimes used to denote units and figure out how they might simplify.

Let's look at the units of energy. The SI units are joules. From looking at the two equations for potential energy, we see that this is equivalent to \[\mathrm{energy} = [\mathrm{kg}][\mathrm{m ~ s^{-2}}][\mathrm{m}].\]

For kinetic energy, \[\mathrm{energy} = [\mathrm{kg}][\mathrm{m ~ s^{-1}}]^2.\] From these two, we see that energy generally simplifies into \(\mathrm{kg ~ m^2 ~ s^{-2}}\). This is equivalent to Joules (\(\mathrm{J}\)), which is the SI unit for energy.
